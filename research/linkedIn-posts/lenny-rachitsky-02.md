**Full Text**: Apply to write a guest post for Lenny's Newsletter

The majority of my most popular posts these days are guest posts, and I realized I haven't had an open call for guest posts in years.

What's a lesson, tactic, framework, or way of working that has been extremely helpful to you in your work that others might find useful?

No prior writing experience is required. I will help you edit and refine your writing. The only requirement is that you've built successful product(s) and have something valuable to share with the world. This will become the best thing you've ever written online.

What I’ll look for in the proposals: 
1. Is it highly actionable and concrete? Can a reader immediately do something with your advice?
2. Is it well-researched and/or rooted in something you are uniquely knowledgeable about?
3. Does this add something new to the conversation about building successful products, careers, or teams?

Turn-offs:
1. You're trying to promote your product/company
2. High on pontification/theory, low on tactics/specifics...
   
**Pattern**: Hook -> Curated Insights -> Framework -> Reflection -> CTA
**Key Insight**: Most people think innovation means creating something completely new. Mark Pincus argues: Most successful products start with something already proven. Then: Find what already works; Make it better; Add one new element. Innovation is often evolutionary before it becomes revolutionary. This reduces risk while preserving upside.
**My Note**: Many people chase originality.
But in reality, success often comes from observing what already works, improving it, and then adding meaningful innovation. The lesson is not “copy others”. The lesson is: Respect evidence before chasing novelty. The same principle applies to hiring, leadership, system improvement, and career growth. The best ideas are not always the newest. The best ideas are often the most effective.
**Repurpose Angle**: (Psychology Angle).
Hook: Why do people often value credentials more than evidence?
Observation: Psychologist call this authority bias. Human naturally trust signals: Degrees; Title; Certifications. Because they are easy to see. Actual competence is harder to measure.
Reflection: That is why organizations sometimes overlook proven performers. They evaluate the signal instead of the outcome.
Closing: The challenge isn’t finding talent. The challenge is recognizing it when it doesn’t arrive wrapped in the credentials we expect.
